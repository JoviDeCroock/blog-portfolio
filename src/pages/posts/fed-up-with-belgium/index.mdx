# Fed up with Belgium

This is not my usual post. No GraphQL, no signals, no VDOM. This one is about
the country I live in, work in, and pay into, because after eight or nine years
of doing exactly that... I'm fed up.

Let me get one thing out of the way first: this isn't a "taxes are theft" rant.
I've spent a large chunk of my career happily in the highest tax bracket and I
honestly did not mind. We have healthcare that doesn't bankrupt you, roads that
mostly work, schools, a safety net. For years I looked at the line on my payslip
that disappeared and told myself that's the deal, and I believed in the deal.
What I've slowly realised is that the deal keeps changing, and it only ever
changes in one direction.

## The hole we dug

Belgium is broke. Not "tightening the belt" broke, structurally broke. Public
debt sits at roughly [107% of GDP, about €692 billion](https://tradingeconomics.com/belgium/government-debt-to-gdp),
and the projections have it drifting _up_ towards 113% by 2027, not down. We
spend around 2.2% of GDP every year just servicing the interest. That's money
that buys nothing, builds nothing, treats no one. It just pays for the fact that
we spent it already.

So what's the plan to fill the hole? As far as I can tell, it's two ideas on a
loop:

- Raise the taxes we already have.
- Invent taxes we didn't have.

That's it. That's the strategy. Not "spend less", not "make the machine
smaller", not "make it easier to build something here". Just... find the next
thing to tax.

## The most taxed labour on the planet

We are not a low-tax country that's nudging rates up. We are, per the OECD's own
[Taxing Wages numbers](https://www.oecd.org/en/blogs/2025/09/how-much-tax-do-workers-pay-oecd-taxing-wages-knows-the-answer.html),
the most heavily taxed labour market in the developed world. Year after year. The
tax wedge for a single worker on average pay is about 52.5%, the highest of all
38 OECD countries, against an OECD average of ~35%. Put differently, the average
Belgian worker takes home around 60% of what they cost. Elsewhere in the OECD,
it is closer to 75%. And the 50% bracket starts biting at a salary a mid-career
engineer clears without trying.

I was in that top bracket for about five of my employed years. I want to be clear
again, I made my peace with that. But you have to understand the starting point
here before anyone in Brussels opens their mouth about people "not contributing
their fair share". We were already number one. There was no headroom being left
on the table. We were already the example every other country points at.

## Meanwhile, on the spending side

The part that turns "fed up" into actual anger: while we're the most taxed
workers on earth, the machine we're funding is... a lot of machine.

We are a country of roughly eleven and a half million people with
[six governments](https://www.fedasilinfo.be/en/belgium-has-6-governments) and,
famously, [nine ministers of health](https://www.brusselstimes.com/news/belgium-all-news/98588/heres-why-belgium-has-nine-health-ministers-regions-communities-powers-responsibilities-coronavirus/).
Nine. Almost one health minister per million inhabitants. Every one of those
governments is a full set of ministers, cabinets, advisors and salaries, for a
country you can drive across in two hours.

At the same time, we have a huge inactivity problem. Unemployment in Brussels
runs north of 12%, Wallonia around 8%, and [Statbel puts the 20-64 employment
rate](https://statbel.fgov.be/en/themes/work-training/employment-and-unemployment)
at 63.9% in Brussels and 67.9% in Wallonia. More than a third of working-age
people in Brussels are not working. In 2025, we were [the last country in the
EU](https://brusselssignal.eu/2025/09/belgium-last-eu-country-to-end-lifetime-entitlement-to-unemployment-benefit/)
to end unlimited-in-time unemployment benefits. The last one.

And the companies that actually generate the wealth to pay for all of it? They're
leaving. [Audi Brussels shut its doors](https://www.brusselstimes.com/1463904/audi-factory-in-brussels-shuts-up-shop-after-more-than-75-years)
in February 2025 after more than 75 years, around 3,000 direct and indirect jobs
gone, the Q8 e-tron moved to Mexico. Van Hool collapsed the year before, another
~1,850 jobs. Volvo in Ghent is now the _last_ major car plant standing in the
whole country, and even that one needs a government task force to keep it here.
We are actively watching our industrial base walk out the door, and the response
is to reach deeper into the pockets of whoever's left.

## And now, capital gains

This year we finally got a
[capital gains tax](https://www.ey.com/en_be/insights/tax/the-new-belgian-capital-gains-tax-what-changes-in-2026),
a 10% "solidarity contribution" on financial assets, live since 1 January 2026,
with a €10,000 yearly exemption. And look, 10% is objectively low compared to
plenty of other countries, I'm not going to pretend it's confiscatory.

But notice what it taxes. This lands on the money you managed not to spend. You
already gave up half of your salary on the way in. You saved what survived. Now,
when that already-taxed money grows, there's a fresh hand out for that too. The
direction of travel is hard to miss: every remaining pocket of "someone here has
a bit of money" gets a new line item. "Solidarity" is a wonderful word for it,
isn't it.

## My actual story

This is where it stops being abstract for me.

I was a regular employee for eight, nine years. Top bracket, no complaints,
covered above. Then I moved to a company where an EOR arrangement wasn't on the
table, so if I wanted the role I had to go freelance. Fine. I registered as a
sole trader, an _eenmanszaak_, and I stayed... right where I'd always been, in
the highest bracket, now paying it personally on business income.

Then I went looking for one of the genuinely nice things this country still
offers the self-employed: buying a car through your business. To actually use
that in any sensible way, you form a company, a BV. So I did.

The part nobody tells you until you're inside it: a BV isn't just about the car.
Once you're a company, the structure genuinely lets you do more.
Your effective rate can drop, you can leave money in the company and reinvest it,
you can pay yourself out over time in more efficient ways. I was actually
_excited_ discovering this. For the first time in my working life it felt like
the system had a door in it that opened towards building something, rather than
just a meter that ran while I worked.

That door did not stay open for long.

## Going after the people who stayed

It turns out the government has decided that people like me forming a BV is a
problem to be solved. Not a behaviour to encourage, but a leak to plug. If your
tax drops because you are using legal paths, that's apparently revenue they
would rather have back. So the [Arizona-coalition measures](https://www.deloitte.com/be/en/services/tax/perspectives/federal-government-major-tax-measures-in-the-arizona-government-agreement.html)
come for exactly that structure, from several directions at once:

- The minimum director's salary you must pay yourself to keep the reduced 20%
  corporate rate goes [from €45,000 to €50,000](https://www.bnpparibasfortis.be/en/public/article/remuneration-dirigeant-arizona),
  indexed upward every year from here.
- Benefits in kind can now make up _at most 20%_ of that remuneration, which
  quietly guts the one benefit that sent me down this road in the first place,
  the car.
- The liquidation reserve, the mechanism for eventually getting your own money
  out, becomes less attractive: the reduced withholding that used to be 5% is
  moving through 6.5% and then to 9.8%.
- The favourable withholding on dividends (VVPRbis) goes from 15% to 18%, and the
  intermediate rate disappears entirely for new contributions.

Read the justification behind it in plain language and it's almost refreshing in
its honesty: the goal is to "curb tax optimisation through management companies",
aimed squarely at "management companies and SMEs where the director takes an
insufficient salary". Which is a bureaucrat's way of describing me. One person.
One or two clients. No employees. A laptop and an invoice.

That's the target. Not the multinational with a Luxembourg holding and a floor
full of tax lawyers. They'll be fine, they're always fine. Me. The freelancer who
finally found the one legal, government-designed door that pointed towards
building, and walked through it a little too visibly.

## The part they never say out loud

Go read [r/BEFreelance](https://www.reddit.com/r/BEFreelance/comments/1ukzb8e/consider_the_end_near/)
and you'll find a whole community that saw this coming. One thing someone wrote
there stuck with me: none of the coverage even _tries_ to show both sides. There
is endless talk about the freelancer's lower effective rate, and total silence on
the risk they carry to earn it. No paid holiday, no sick pay, no notice period,
no unemployment safety net, you eat the gap between contracts yourself, you carry
the liability yourself.

And the whole crackdown rests on an assumption that falls apart the second you
poke it: that if a freelancer billing €137k a year is pushed off this structure,
they simply become an employee earning €137k a year, and the treasury collects
the difference. That's not what happens. That job at that number frequently doesn't
exist on payroll, so best case the state collects far less than it imagined, and
worst case it collects _nothing_, because the person just... leaves.

And that is what I'm starting to see. The higher earners are already doing the
maths. Some are chasing international clients so their income no longer depends
on this place at all. Some are quietly looking at where to move. More
opportunity, higher rates, lower taxes, and a government that isn't redesigning
its rules every eighteen months specifically to catch _them_. When your most
productive, most mobile people start treating your country as a problem to route
around, that's not a loophole closing. That's a warning.

---

I want to end where I started, because I don't want this to read like it came
from someone who never wanted to pay in. I did. For the better part of a decade
I paid the highest labour taxes in the developed world and genuinely believed in
the trade. I still believe in the idea behind it: healthcare, education, a floor
under people. That is worth funding. I'll fund it.

But you cannot run the most expensive labour market on earth, staff six
governments and nine health ministers on top of it, watch your factories close
one by one, and then decide the real problem, the pocket that finally needs
emptying, is the one-person company with a laptop and two clients. That isn't
solidarity. It's eating the seed corn and calling it a harvest.

The hole is real and it does need filling. But you don't fill it by chasing off
the last people still willing to build something here. And more of us are
noticing that the cheapest way to stop being the target... is to stop being
here.

That's the rant. I wish it felt less rational.
